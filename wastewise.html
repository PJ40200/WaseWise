Life is a Waste

Today we live in a world of waste ! Yes that's true India annually needs land equivalent to ten times the area of Rashtrapati Bhawan estate to dump waste that urban areas generate.Open and unsanitary landfills contribute to contamination of drinking water and can cause infection and transmit diseases. The dispersal of debris pollutes ecosystems and dangerous substances from electronic waste or industrial garbage puts a strain on the health of urban dwellers and the environment

Recycling is the process of collecting and processing materials that would otherwise be thrown away as trash, turning them into new products. Instead of disposing of items like paper, plastic, glass, and metal in landfills or incinerators, recycling allows these materials to be used again to create new products.
These stuff can be recycled Paper Products: Newspapers,Magazines, Office paper, Junk mail, Cardboard boxes (broken down flat) Plastic Containers: Plastic bottles (water bottles, soda bottles), Plastic jugs (milk jugs, detergent bottles), Plastic containers (yogurt cups, margarine tubs), Plastic lids and caps (if accepted, often they need to be removed and recycled separately) Glass: Glass bottles, Glass jars (jam jars, sauce jars) Metal: Aluminum cans (soda cans, beer cans), Steel cans (soup cans, vegetable cans), Tin cans Cardboard: Shipping boxes, Cereal boxes, Food boxes (frozen food boxes), Cartons: Milk cartons, Juice boxes Electronics (often through specialized programs): Cell phones, Computers, Batteries, Printers

While we can;t recycle Plastic Bags and Wrappers: Plastic shopping bags, Chip bags, Candy wrappers Styrofoam: Foam packaging, Takeout containers Broken Glass: Broken drinking glasses, Broken window glass Food Waste: Food scraps, Leftovers Bulbs: Incandescent bulbs, CFL bulbs (compact fluorescent lights) Hazardous Waste: Paint cans, Pesticides, Chemical cleaners Composite Materials: Items made of mixed materials that are hard to separate, like juice boxes with foil linings

Composting

1. What is Composting?

Composting is a natural process where organic materials decompose into a nutrient-rich soil conditioner. It's a way of recycling organic waste like food scraps, yard trimmings, and paper into a valuable resource for gardening and agriculture.

2. Compostable Items

  • Green Materials: Examples include fruit and vegetable scraps, coffee grounds, grass clippings, and plant trimmings.
  • Brown Materials: Examples include leaves, straw, wood chips, and newspaper.

3. Items to Avoid

Avoid composting meat, dairy, oils, pet waste, and diseased plants. Special considerations should be made for items like eggshells, cardboard, and tea bags.

4. Breakdown of Composting Process

  1. Organic Materials: Start with a mix of "green" materials (like fruit and vegetable scraps, grass clippings) and "brown" materials (such as leaves, straw, paper). The green provides nitrogen, while the brown adds carbon.
  2. Microorganisms: Bacteria, fungi, worms, and other tiny organisms break down the organic matter. They need air, water, and the right balance of green and brown materials to thrive.
  3. Decomposition: Over time, these microorganisms break down the organic materials into a dark, crumbly substance called humus. This humus is rich in nutrients and beneficial for plants.
  4. Finished Compost: When the composting process is complete, you're left with nutrient-rich compost that can be added to soil to improve its structure, fertility, and ability to retain moisture.
